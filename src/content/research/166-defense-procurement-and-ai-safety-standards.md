## Introduction

The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into defense systems presents both significant opportunities and critical challenges. As militaries worldwide adopt AI to enhance operational capabilities, the need for robust safety standards becomes paramount. Defense procurement—the processes by which governments acquire military technologies—plays a pivotal role in shaping the development, deployment, and oversight of AI systems. These procurement decisions influence not only military efficacy but also the safety, accountability, and compliance of AI systems with international norms.

This article explores defense procurement as a critical lever for embedding AI safety standards into military applications. It examines the dual-use nature of many AI technologies, the unique pressures of the defense sector, and the interplay between procurement policies and broader AI governance frameworks. Finally, it offers recommendations to ensure that defense procurement aligns with long-term AI safety objectives.

## The Dual-Use Challenge: AI in Military and Civilian Contexts

Many AI technologies exhibit dual-use characteristics: they are developed for civilian purposes but can also be adapted for military applications. For instance, advances in autonomous navigation for commercial drones are easily transferable to military-grade unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs). Similarly, natural language processing models designed for customer service can be repurposed for intelligence analysis or psychological operations.

This dual-use nature complicates the establishment of safety standards. Civilian regulatory frameworks often fail to account for the unique risks posed by military adaptations, such as the potential for misuse in autonomous weapon systems or vulnerabilities to adversarial attacks. Conversely, defense procurement processes may prioritize operational effectiveness over safety, particularly in high-stakes scenarios where rapid deployment is critical.

The lack of alignment between civilian and military safety standards can result in fragmented governance. For example, while civilian sectors may adopt incident reporting mechanisms for AI failures (see [Incident Databases: Standardizing AI Harm Reporting](/research/142-incident-databases-standardizing-ai-harm-reporting)), similar transparency is often absent in military contexts due to security concerns. Addressing this gap requires a concerted effort to harmonize safety standards across both domains while accounting for their unique constraints.

## Safety by Design in Defense Procurement

Defense procurement processes offer a unique opportunity to embed AI safety standards at the earliest stages of technology development. By incorporating "safety by design" principles into procurement contracts, governments can ensure that AI systems meet rigorous safety, reliability, and ethical standards before they are deployed.

### Key Mechanisms for Embedding Safety

1. **Pre-procurement Requirements**: Governments can mandate that vendors demonstrate compliance with predefined safety standards as a condition for bidding on defense contracts. These standards might include robust testing for adversarial robustness, explainability, and fail-safe mechanisms.
2. **Lifecycle Accountability**: Contracts should require ongoing monitoring and periodic reassessment of AI systems post-deployment. This ensures that safety measures remain effective as systems are updated or exposed to new operational environments.
3. **Third-Party Audits**: Independent audits can verify compliance with safety standards, reducing the risk of conflicts of interest between defense contractors and government agencies.

For instance, the U.S. Department of Defense's "Responsible AI Strategy and Implementation Pathway" emphasizes the importance of ethics and safety throughout the AI lifecycle. However, critics argue that such frameworks often lack enforceable mechanisms to hold contractors accountable. Strengthening these mechanisms through procurement policies could bridge the gap between aspirational principles and practical implementation.

## International Humanitarian Law and Autonomous Systems

The deployment of AI-enabled autonomous systems in warfare raises profound questions under international humanitarian law (IHL). Key principles of IHL—such as distinction, proportionality, and necessity—must be upheld even as AI systems assume greater roles in decision-making.

### The Role of Meaningful Human Control

One of the most contentious issues in this domain is the concept of "meaningful human control." This principle asserts that human operators must retain ultimate authority over decisions to use lethal force. However, ensuring meaningful human control becomes increasingly challenging as AI systems become more autonomous and capable of making complex decisions in real time.

Defense procurement policies can play a vital role in operationalizing meaningful human control. For example, contracts for autonomous systems can include requirements for human-in-the-loop or human-on-the-loop mechanisms, as well as safeguards to prevent misuse or unintended escalation. These measures are discussed in greater detail in [Meaningful Human Control: Operationalizing the Standard](/research/162-meaningful-human-control-operationalizing-the-stan).

### Limitations of Current Frameworks

Current IHL frameworks are often reactive rather than proactive. They address violations after they occur rather than preventing them through robust design and procurement practices. As a result, there is a pressing need for international cooperation to establish binding safety standards for AI-enabled military systems. This aligns with broader efforts to address governance gaps in areas such as [Autonomous Weapons: The State of Treaty Negotiations](/research/161-autonomous-weapons-the-state-of-treaty-negotiation).

## Procurement as a Governance Tool

Defense procurement is not merely a transactional process: it is a governance tool that can shape the trajectory of AI development. By prioritizing safety, ethics, and transparency in procurement policies, governments can influence the behavior of contractors and set industry-wide standards.

### Incentivizing Compliance

One effective strategy is to tie compliance with safety standards to financial incentives. For instance, contracts could include milestone payments contingent on the successful implementation of safety measures. Alternatively, governments could offer tax incentives or expedited approval processes for contractors that exceed baseline safety requirements.

### International Collaboration

Given the global nature of AI development, unilateral approaches to procurement are insufficient. International collaboration is essential to prevent a "race to the bottom" in safety standards. Multilateral agreements, such as those facilitated by the United Nations or NATO, could establish minimum safety requirements for AI systems procured by member states.

## Challenges and Limitations

Despite its potential, the use of defense procurement as a lever for AI safety faces several challenges:

1. **Technical Uncertainty**: The rapid pace of AI advancement makes it difficult to predict future risks and establish appropriate safety standards.
2. **Security Concerns**: The classified nature of many defense projects limits transparency and complicates independent oversight.
3. **Industry Resistance**: Contractors may resist stringent safety requirements, citing increased costs or delays in deployment.

Addressing these challenges will require a combination of technical innovation, policy reform, and international cooperation. For example, the development of standardized testing protocols for AI safety could reduce uncertainty and streamline compliance processes, as explored in [Benchmarking AI Safety: Beyond Capability Evaluations](/research/144-benchmarking-ai-safety-beyond-capability-evaluatio).

## Conclusion

Defense procurement represents a powerful but underutilized tool for advancing AI safety in military and dual-use technologies. By embedding safety standards into procurement processes, governments can ensure that AI systems are developed and deployed responsibly, ethically, and in compliance with international norms. However, realizing this potential will require overcoming significant technical, political, and organizational challenges.

Ultimately, the stakes are too high to ignore. As AI becomes increasingly integral to defense operations, the failure to prioritize safety could have catastrophic consequences—not only for military personnel but also for global stability. By aligning procurement policies with AI safety objectives, we have an opportunity to mitigate these risks and establish a foundation for secure and ethical AI development.

*This article is limited in scope to procurement processes at the national level and does not address subnational or private-sector procurement. Further research is needed to examine these dimensions.*
