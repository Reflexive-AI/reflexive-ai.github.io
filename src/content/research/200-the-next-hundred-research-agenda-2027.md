## Introduction

The rapid pace of artificial intelligence (AI) development has created both unprecedented opportunities and equally significant risks. Over the next century, AI will increasingly shape every dimension of human life: from governance and economics to ethics and environmental sustainability. The year 2027 will be a pivotal moment in this trajectory, as the world moves further into an era of large-scale AI deployment and integration.

This article proposes a research agenda for 2027, outlining the critical questions, tensions, and areas of investigation that will define the next phase of AI governance and safety. By framing our agenda around the concept of "The Next Hundred," we aim to address both the technological advancements (e.g., the next 100 breakthroughs) and the time horizon (the next 100 years of societal transformation). This agenda builds on existing frameworks while identifying gaps in research, policy, and practice.

## 1. Anticipating Transformative AI: The Frontier Problem

The development of advanced AI systems capable of outperforming human cognitive tasks in a broad range of domains—often referred to as "transformative AI"—is a recurring concern in governance and safety circles. The prospect of such systems raises critical questions about risk management, global coordination, and ethical guardrails. 

### Defining Transformative AI  
To frame the research agenda, we must first clarify what we mean by transformative AI. This refers to systems with the potential to significantly disrupt economic, political, and social structures. Given the inherent uncertainty surrounding these systems, even defining them is a challenge. A key priority for researchers in 2027 will be to develop operational definitions, akin to the way [AI System Self-Declaration Format](/research/193-ai-system-self-declaration-format) proposes standardizing system capabilities.

### Key Research Questions  
1. How can we develop early warning systems to detect the emergence of transformative AI?  
2. What mechanisms can ensure that transformative AI development is globally coordinated rather than competitive?  
3. How can we establish and enforce global norms for transparency and accountability in high-stakes AI systems?  

The notion of "governance before deployment" will be central to this discourse. Precedents such as nuclear arms control and biosecurity offer valuable historical lessons, yet their application to AI remains fraught with challenges, as highlighted in [Confidence-Building Measures for Military AI](/research/170-confidence-building-measures-for-military-ai).

## 2. AI and Climate: Dual Crises, Dual Solutions

The climate crisis and AI's environmental impact are two intertwined challenges that must be addressed in tandem. While AI holds enormous potential to optimize resource use and model environmental scenarios, its energy consumption and resource demands also exacerbate ecological degradation.

### Mitigating AI's Carbon and Resource Footprint  
AI systems, especially large-scale models, require significant computational resources. This has led to a growing interest in the environmental implications of AI, as explored in [The Carbon Footprint of AI: Measurement and Disclosure](/research/172-the-carbon-footprint-of-ai-measurement-and-disclos) and [Water Consumption of AI Data Centers](/research/179-water-consumption-of-ai-data-centers). In 2027, research must focus on:  
- Developing more energy-efficient AI architectures.  
- Establishing standardized metrics for environmental impact assessments.  
- Incentivizing the adoption of sustainable practices in AI deployment.  

### AI as a Tool for Climate Adaptation  
Simultaneously, AI can play a transformative role in mitigating climate change. From predicting extreme weather patterns to optimizing renewable energy grids, the potential applications are vast. As noted in [AI and Climate Adaptation: Governance Framework](/research/171-ai-and-climate-adaptation-governance-framework), governance frameworks must ensure that AI is deployed equitably and responsibly to address global climate challenges.

## 3. The Role of AI in Its Own Regulation

A defining feature of AI is its capacity for self-improvement and self-monitoring, which presents unique opportunities—and challenges—for governance. By 2027, we foresee a growing reliance on AI systems to assist in their own regulation, whether through self-auditing mechanisms, automated compliance tools, or dynamic governance protocols.

### Opportunities in AI Self-Regulation  
AI-driven regulatory frameworks could streamline compliance and enforcement. For instance, the [Regulatory API Specification](/research/194-regulatory-api-specification) demonstrates how machine-readable standards could enable real-time monitoring and reporting. Future research must explore how to:  
- Design AI systems capable of self-assessment against legal and ethical criteria.  
- Use AI to identify and mitigate risks in other AI systems, particularly in high-stakes domains like healthcare and finance.  
- Ensure that AI self-regulation systems are robust, transparent, and resistant to adversarial manipulation.  

### Challenges and Risks  
However, delegating regulatory functions to AI systems raises several concerns. These include the risk of codifying biases into regulatory processes and the potential for over-reliance on opaque systems. Research must investigate how to balance human oversight with the efficiency gains of AI-driven governance.

## 4. Equity, Power, and Inclusion in AI Governance

As AI reshapes global power structures, it is essential to ensure that its benefits and risks are distributed equitably. The concentration of AI development in a few countries and corporations creates risks of geopolitical tension and economic disparity.

### Global Coordination  
The international community must prioritize inclusive governance frameworks to prevent the monopolization of AI benefits. Research on [Industry Consortia for AI Safety: Comparative Analysis](/research/190-industry-consortia-for-ai-safety-comparative-analy) highlights the potential of multistakeholder approaches, but challenges remain in ensuring equitable representation for developing nations.  

### Addressing Algorithmic Inequities  
AI systems often encode and exacerbate biases, disproportionately harming marginalized communities. Future research must focus on:  
- Developing methods to detect and mitigate bias in AI systems.  
- Understanding the long-term social impacts of AI-driven decision-making.  
- Creating governance structures that prioritize inclusivity and fairness.  

## 5. The Convergence of AI and Emerging Technologies

AI does not exist in isolation. Its convergence with other technologies—such as neurotechnology, biotechnology, and quantum computing—will amplify both its capabilities and its risks. For example, the intersection of AI and neurotechnology, as discussed in [AI-Neurotechnology Convergence: Governance Implications](/research/198-ai-neurotechnology-convergence-governance-implicat), raises profound ethical and regulatory questions.

### Research Priorities  
1. How can we govern the interactions between AI and other transformative technologies?  
2. What frameworks are needed to address the unique risks posed by these convergences?  
3. How can we ensure that these technologies are developed in ways that align with societal values and priorities?  

The integration of AI with other technologies will require a multidisciplinary approach, drawing on expertise from fields like bioethics, cybersecurity, and international law.

## Conclusion

The year 2027 represents a critical juncture in the development and governance of AI. By addressing the key research priorities outlined in this agenda—anticipating transformative AI, mitigating environmental impacts, leveraging AI for self-regulation, ensuring equity, and governing technological convergence—we can navigate the next hundred developments with foresight and responsibility.

This agenda is not exhaustive but serves as a foundation for future inquiry. As the field evolves, continuous iteration and collaboration will be essential. Reflexive governance—where AI systems, researchers, and policymakers learn and adapt in real time—must be at the heart of our approach.

*The scope of this article is limited to broad research priorities and does not address specific implementation strategies. Future work should focus on operationalizing these priorities in diverse contexts.*
