## Introduction

The rapid expansion of artificial intelligence (AI) and its associated infrastructure has brought about significant environmental challenges. While the energy consumption and carbon footprint of AI systems have received considerable attention (see [The Carbon Footprint of AI: Measurement and Disclosure](/research/172-the-carbon-footprint-of-ai-measurement-and-disclos)), water consumption by data centers remains an underexplored yet critical issue. Water is essential for cooling servers in data centers, which are the backbone of AI operations. As AI models grow in complexity, requiring enormous computational resources, the demand for water-intensive infrastructure is increasing. This article examines the water usage of AI data centers, its environmental consequences, and governance challenges.

Understanding and mitigating the water impact of AI is vital for sustainable development. Without targeted policies, water consumption could exacerbate resource scarcity in vulnerable communities, particularly those already facing climate-induced water stress. This article explores these dynamics, offering recommendations for aligning AI infrastructure with environmental sustainability.

## Why Data Centers Consume Water

AI data centers are facilities that house the computational hardware necessary to train and deploy AI models. These servers generate substantial heat during operation, necessitating efficient cooling systems to maintain functionality and prevent overheating. Water-based cooling systems are among the most efficient and widely used methods, often requiring millions of gallons annually.

### Cooling Systems and Water Use

The cooling systems in data centers typically rely on evaporative cooling towers, where water absorbs heat from the servers and then evaporates. This process typically uses two types of water:

1. **Direct Operational Water Use:** Water used directly in cooling towers or other infrastructure.
2. **Indirect Virtual Water Use:** Water indirectly consumed through electricity production, especially if the energy grid relies on water-intensive power sources such as coal or nuclear energy.

While air-based cooling systems exist, they are less efficient and often impractical for large-scale AI operations. As a result, water-intensive cooling remains the industry standard for hyperscale data centers, which are often used to train advanced AI models.

### AI Workloads and Growing Demand

AI workloads are computationally intensive, far more so than traditional data processing tasks. Training a single large-scale AI model can take weeks or months, requiring thousands of interconnected GPUs and TPUs, all running continuously. For instance, training GPT-3, a well-known large language model, is estimated to have consumed the energy equivalent of hundreds of thousands of kilowatt-hours. The associated water consumption for cooling such operations is similarly immense.

## The Scale of Water Consumption

Quantifying the water footprint of AI data centers is difficult due to limited transparency in reporting. However, estimates suggest that large-scale data centers can consume between 1 and 5 million gallons of water per day. For context, this is comparable to the daily water needs of a small city.

### Regional Variations in Impact

The environmental impact of water consumption varies by region. In water-rich areas, the burden may be manageable, but data centers are often located in regions with cheap electricity, such as the southwestern United States, where water scarcity is a growing concern. For example:

- **Arizona:** Several hyperscale data centers operate in this arid state, drawing water from already stressed aquifers.
- **California:** The state’s high energy demands, combined with frequent droughts, make water use for cooling especially contentious.
- **India:** Rapid AI adoption in India is creating demand for new data centers, but the country faces ongoing water scarcity issues that could worsen with unregulated expansion.

These regional disparities highlight the need for localized governance frameworks that account for water availability and climate conditions.

## Environmental and Social Implications

The water consumption of AI data centers has far-reaching consequences for ecosystems, communities, and global sustainability. 

### Ecosystem Disruption

Large-scale water withdrawal can disrupt local ecosystems, particularly in areas where rivers, lakes, or aquifers are the primary water sources. Reduced water levels can harm aquatic life, disrupt agriculture, and reduce biodiversity. These effects are amplified in regions experiencing climate change, where water resources are already under stress.

### Community Impact

Data centers often compete with local communities for limited water resources, creating tension. In some cases, the prioritization of industrial water use over community needs has led to public protests. For example, in Chile’s Atacama Desert, community groups have voiced concerns over the water-intensive operations of lithium mining, a parallel issue that illustrates the risks of resource misallocation.

### Climate Feedback Loops

Water-intensive AI infrastructure can exacerbate climate feedback loops. For instance, reliance on water for cooling in regions experiencing droughts may force facilities to switch to less efficient cooling methods, increasing energy consumption and carbon emissions. This creates a vicious cycle where AI infrastructure contributes to climate change, which in turn exacerbates water scarcity.

## Governance Challenges

Despite the critical need for regulation, water consumption by AI data centers remains poorly governed. Existing frameworks focus primarily on carbon emissions, neglecting water as a resource that requires equal attention.

### Lack of Reporting Standards

One of the primary governance gaps is the absence of standardized reporting requirements for water usage. While some companies voluntarily disclose water consumption, these disclosures are often incomplete or inconsistent. Mandating transparency, similar to carbon footprint disclosures, would be a key step toward accountability.

### Incentives for Water-Efficient Design

Current market incentives do little to encourage water-efficient AI infrastructure. Government subsidies or tax incentives for adopting alternative cooling methods—such as liquid immersion cooling—could help reduce water dependence. However, such incentives must be coupled with robust enforcement to prevent greenwashing.

### International Coordination

Water governance is inherently local but increasingly interconnected. For example, the global supply chain for AI hardware may shift water burdens across borders. International standards for sustainable AI infrastructure could promote best practices while addressing disparities between water-rich and water-scarce regions. Cross-referencing frameworks like the UN Sustainable Development Goals could provide a basis for such standards.

## Recommendations for Sustainable AI Infrastructure

To mitigate the water impact of AI data centers, we propose several actionable strategies:

1. **Mandatory Water Footprint Reporting:** Policymakers should require data centers to disclose their water consumption, both direct and virtual, as part of broader environmental reporting.
   
2. **Investment in Alternative Cooling Technologies:** Governments and private companies should prioritize research and development into non-water-intensive cooling solutions, such as liquid immersion or air-based systems.

3. **Location-Specific Regulations:** Regional water management policies should align with local resource availability, ensuring that data centers do not exacerbate water scarcity in vulnerable areas.

4. **Cross-Sector Collaboration:** AI developers, data center operators, and environmental groups should work together to establish industry-wide sustainability standards.

5. **Public Awareness Campaigns:** Educating the public about the water impact of AI and data centers can help build support for sustainable governance measures.

## Conclusion

The water consumption of AI data centers represents an urgent environmental concern that intersects with global challenges such as climate change and resource scarcity. As AI infrastructure continues to expand, addressing this issue will require coordinated efforts across governments, industries, and civil society. By implementing robust governance structures and investing in innovative cooling technologies, we can ensure that the growth of AI aligns with broader sustainability goals.

*This analysis focuses on water consumption from cooling systems and does not account for other water-related impacts of AI infrastructure, such as water used in hardware manufacturing or indirect water use in renewable energy production.*
