## Introduction

The integrity of elections is foundational to democracy. Advances in artificial intelligence (AI) have introduced both opportunities and risks to electoral systems worldwide. While AI can enhance voter engagement, improve administrative efficiency, and facilitate transparency, its misuse threatens democratic processes. From disinformation campaigns to algorithmic biases in voter outreach, the scope of AI's impact on elections requires a systematic understanding to enable effective governance. This article outlines a taxonomy of threats posed by AI in elections, supported by evidence and case studies, and concludes with recommendations for mitigating these risks.

## The Role of AI in Electoral Systems

AI technologies are increasingly integrated into elections, ranging from voter registration systems to political campaigning tools. These integrations aim to streamline processes and improve accessibility, yet they also present vulnerabilities. For example:

1. **Automated Content Generation:** Political campaigns use AI to create personalized messages at scale, targeting specific demographics with tailored content. While this can increase engagement, it also opens pathways for manipulative practices such as microtargeting based on sensitive data.
2. **Election Administration:** AI-powered systems are used to manage voter databases, predict turnout, and optimize polling logistics. However, reliance on such systems introduces risks related to algorithmic errors or cyberattacks.
3. **Detection and Combatting of Misinformation:** AI tools are deployed to identify fake news and deepfakes, yet these systems are imperfect and can themselves be exploited, as malicious actors devise strategies to evade detection.

While these applications offer promise, the risks are significant. A comprehensive taxonomy is essential to understand and address these threats.

## Categories of AI-Driven Threats in Elections

### 1. **Disinformation and Propaganda**

AI enables the creation of convincing fake news, deepfakes, and synthetic media that can distort public opinion or discredit candidates. Generative AI tools, such as large language models, are capable of producing realistic narratives that spread rapidly online. For instance, during the 2020 U.S. presidential election, researchers identified coordinated disinformation campaigns that leveraged AI to amplify conspiracy theories.

Key mechanisms include:
- **Deepfakes:** AI-generated videos can depict candidates saying or doing things they never did, undermining trust.
- **Automated Troll Networks:** Bots powered by AI amplify divisive content to polarize voters.
- **Algorithmic Amplification:** Social media platforms use recommendation algorithms that inadvertently prioritize sensational or misleading content, exacerbating its reach.

### 2. **Microtargeting and Voter Manipulation**

Political campaigns increasingly use AI to analyze vast datasets, enabling hyper-specific targeting of voters. This includes:
- **Psychographic Profiling:** AI analyzes individual behaviors, preferences, and emotions to craft persuasive messages. The Cambridge Analytica scandal highlighted the dangers of such practices.
- **Manipulative Advertising:** AI-generated ads can exploit cognitive biases to steer voter decision-making, often without transparency about funding or intent.

While microtargeting can increase voter engagement, its misuse raises ethical and governance concerns. Campaigns may prioritize manipulation over persuasion, undermining informed decision-making.

### 3. **Cybersecurity Vulnerabilities**

AI systems in electoral infrastructure, such as voter databases and electronic voting machines, are susceptible to cyberattacks. Threat actors may exploit vulnerabilities to disrupt elections or alter outcomes. Notable risks include:
- **AI-Augmented Cyberattacks:** Hackers use AI to identify security weaknesses or automate large-scale attacks.
- **Data Poisoning:** Adversaries manipulate training data for AI systems, compromising their reliability.
- **Election Interference:** Coordinated attacks on electoral infrastructure can erode trust in democratic institutions.

Cross-referencing with [Incident Databases: Standardizing AI Harm Reporting](/research/142-incident-databases-standardizing-ai-harm-reporting) highlights the importance of documenting AI-related incidents in elections to improve governance.

### 4. **Algorithmic Bias and Discrimination**

AI systems used in voter outreach or administrative processes may introduce bias, unintentionally disenfranchising certain groups. For example:
- **Biased Models:** AI trained on incomplete or biased data may perpetuate systemic inequalities, such as excluding minority groups from voter rolls.
- **Unequal Access:** Automated systems may favor voters with digital literacy while alienating others, exacerbating existing disparities.

This aligns with broader concerns explored in [Language Model Bias Against Low-Resource Languages](/research/134-language-model-bias-against-low-resource-languages), which examines how biased AI systems impact underrepresented communities.

### 5. **Autonomous Decision-Making in Governance**

AI-driven decision-making tools are increasingly used to manage electoral logistics, such as polling station assignments or ballot counting. However, delegating critical decisions to AI poses risks:
- **Accountability Issues:** Who is responsible when AI systems make errors?
- **Lack of Transparency:** Black-box models used in electoral administration may obscure decision-making processes, reducing public trust.

These challenges underscore the need for robust governance frameworks, as discussed in [Measuring AI Governance Effectiveness](/research/141-measuring-ai-governance-effectiveness).

## Mitigation Strategies

Responding to these risks requires proactive measures from governments, international organizations, and civil society. Key recommendations include:

1. **Regulatory Frameworks:** Governments should establish regulations governing AI use in electoral systems, focusing on transparency, accountability, and fairness.
2. **Public Awareness Campaigns:** Educating voters about AI risks, such as disinformation, can reduce susceptibility to manipulation.
3. **Technical Safeguards:** Investments in cybersecurity, bias detection, and model auditing are essential to safeguard electoral infrastructure.
4. **Multilateral Cooperation:** International bodies, such as the UN, should coordinate efforts to combat cross-border AI-driven election interference.

## The Global Governance Challenge

Addressing AI risks in elections requires global coordination. While some countries have implemented policies regulating AI in political campaigns, others lag behind. Disparities in governance create opportunities for exploitation, particularly in regions with weaker institutional frameworks. For instance, the misuse of AI to influence elections in developing democracies has been documented, yet these nations often lack the resources to respond effectively.

Cross-referencing [AI Governance in Sub-Saharan Africa: Current State](/research/131-ai-governance-in-sub-saharan-africa-current-state) illustrates the challenges faced by low-income countries in adapting to AI risks.

## Conclusion

AI introduces profound risks to electoral integrity, spanning disinformation, voter manipulation, cybersecurity vulnerabilities, algorithmic bias, and governance challenges. These threats undermine democratic processes and erode public trust. A proactive approach—grounded in evidence-based policymaking, international cooperation, and robust technical safeguards—is essential to mitigate these risks and ensure AI serves democracy rather than subverting it.

*This article focuses on high-level categories of AI-related threats to elections and does not address specific technical aspects of AI implementation or detailed case studies. Further research is needed to develop granular, region-specific mitigation strategies.*
