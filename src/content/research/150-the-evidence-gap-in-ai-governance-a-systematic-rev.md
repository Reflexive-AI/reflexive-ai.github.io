## Introduction

As artificial intelligence (AI) systems grow increasingly pervasive, questions about their governance have become urgent. Policymakers, regulators, and civil society organizations are grappling with how to design effective frameworks to ensure AI systems are safe, fair, and aligned with societal goals. However, a critical challenge has emerged: the lack of robust, empirical evidence on which to base governance decisions. This evidence gap, defined as the absence of adequate data, methodologies, and standardized metrics to evaluate AI systems and their societal impact, threatens the development of effective regulatory regimes.

This systematic review investigates the evidence gap in AI governance, examining its origins, implications, and potential remedies. By synthesizing existing research and providing cross-sectoral insights, this article highlights the urgent need for evidence-based approaches to ensure the safe and equitable deployment of AI technologies.

## The Evidence Gap: Defining the Problem

### What is the Evidence Gap?

The evidence gap in AI governance refers to the disparity between the demand for reliable, actionable data to inform policy decisions and the availability of such evidence. This gap spans multiple dimensions, including:

1. **Technical Metrics:** The lack of consensus on how to measure key attributes of AI systems, such as fairness, transparency, and safety.
2. **Impact Assessments:** Limited longitudinal studies that track the societal and economic effects of AI deployment over time (see [Longitudinal Studies of AI Deployment Effects](/research/149-longitudinal-studies-of-ai-deployment-effects)).
3. **Harm Reporting:** Inconsistent reporting and documentation of AI-related harms, as explored in [Incident Databases: Standardizing AI Harm Reporting](/research/142-incident-databases-standardizing-ai-harm-reporting).

This lack of evidence is not merely a technical or academic issue: it directly impacts the effectiveness of governance. Without robust evidence, regulations risk being either overly prescriptive and stifling innovation or too lenient and failing to mitigate harms.

### Causes of the Evidence Gap

Several factors contribute to the evidence gap in AI governance:

- **Rapid Technological Change:** The speed of AI development often outpaces the ability of researchers and policymakers to study its impacts. Emerging technologies like generative AI and agentic systems introduce novel risks that are poorly understood (see [Plain Language Guide to Agentic AI Risks](/research/120-plain-language-guide-to-agentic-ai-risks)).
- **Proprietary Constraints:** Much of the data necessary for governance is controlled by private companies. Trade secrets, intellectual property concerns, and competitive pressures limit the availability of information for public scrutiny.
- **Methodological Challenges:** Evaluating AI systems often requires complex, interdisciplinary approaches that integrate insights from computer science, social science, and law. Existing methodologies are often inadequate for capturing these complexities.
- **Global Disparities:** Evidence gaps are particularly pronounced in low-resource settings, where digital infrastructure and research capacity may lag behind (see [Digital Infrastructure Gaps and AI Readiness](/research/135-digital-infrastructure-gaps-and-ai-readiness)).

## Implications of the Evidence Gap

### Policy Effectiveness and Legitimacy

The absence of evidence undermines the legitimacy and effectiveness of AI governance. Policies that are perceived as arbitrary or uninformed risk losing public trust, which is essential for compliance and enforcement. For example, poorly designed regulations may fail to address real-world risks, leaving vulnerable communities exposed to harm. Conversely, overly restrictive policies may stifle innovation, depriving society of the potential benefits of AI.

### Unequal Impacts

The evidence gap exacerbates inequality. Without data to inform inclusive policy design, AI governance risks reinforcing existing biases and disparities. For instance, the lack of data on the impact of AI on marginalized groups limits the ability to develop targeted interventions, as discussed in [Language Model Bias Against Low-Resource Languages](/research/134-language-model-bias-against-low-resource-languages).

### Barriers to International Cooperation

AI is a global phenomenon, but governance frameworks are often fragmented along national or regional lines. The lack of standardized metrics and shared data complicates efforts to coordinate international regulations. This is particularly problematic for addressing cross-border issues like AI-driven surveillance, as highlighted in [AI-Driven Surveillance in Authoritarian Contexts](/research/138-ai-driven-surveillance-in-authoritarian-contexts).

## Strategies to Address the Evidence Gap

### Standardizing Metrics and Reporting

Developing standardized metrics for evaluating AI systems is a critical first step. Initiatives like model cards and datasheets for datasets represent promising efforts to create consistent documentation practices. However, these tools must evolve to address complex, high-risk systems. A proportional approach, as discussed in [Operationalizing Proportionality in Model Disclosure](/research/001-operationalizing-proportionality-in-model-disclosure), could ensure that transparency requirements scale with the potential impact of the system.

### Investment in Longitudinal Studies

Longitudinal studies are essential for understanding the long-term societal and economic impacts of AI. These studies should prioritize diverse geographic and demographic contexts to ensure that findings are globally relevant. For example, research on AI's role in development, such as [AI and Agricultural Development in Low-Income Countries](/research/133-ai-and-agricultural-development-in-low-income-coun), highlights the importance of context-specific insights.

### Public-Private Partnerships

Collaboration between the public and private sectors can help bridge the evidence gap. Governments can incentivize companies to share data and insights, while ensuring that intellectual property and privacy concerns are addressed. Initiatives like incident reporting databases could serve as a model for such partnerships.

### Capacity Building in Low-Resource Settings

Closing the evidence gap requires investment in research capacity, particularly in low-resource settings. This includes funding for local researchers, infrastructure development, and international collaboration. The importance of such efforts is underscored in [South-South AI Governance Cooperation](/research/137-south-south-ai-governance-cooperation), which examines how countries in the Global South can collaborate to build governance capacity.

## The Role of Reflexive Governance

Reflexive governance, which emphasizes iterative learning and adaptation, offers a promising framework for addressing the evidence gap. By integrating mechanisms for continuous evaluation and feedback, reflexive governance can help policymakers respond to new evidence as it emerges. This approach aligns with the principles of adaptive regulation and has been suggested as a way to manage the uncertainties associated with rapidly evolving technologies.

## Conclusion

The evidence gap in AI governance represents a significant challenge, but it is not insurmountable. By standardizing metrics, investing in longitudinal studies, fostering public-private partnerships, and building capacity in low-resource settings, we can begin to close this gap. Reflexive governance offers a pathway to ensure that policies remain adaptive and evidence-based, enabling the safe and equitable deployment of AI technologies.

As AI continues to reshape society, the need for robust, evidence-based governance will only grow. Addressing the evidence gap is not merely a technical necessity: it is a moral imperative to ensure that the benefits of AI are shared equitably and its risks are responsibly managed.
