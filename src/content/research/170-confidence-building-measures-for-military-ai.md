## Introduction

The rapid integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into military systems has introduced new capabilities and strategic opportunities, but also significant risks. The development and deployment of military AI systems, including autonomous weapons, surveillance systems, and AI-enabled logistics, are raising concerns about unintended escalations, accidents, and the erosion of international stability. In this context, **confidence-building measures (CBMs)**—mechanisms designed to reduce mistrust and foster transparency between states—are increasingly relevant for mitigating risks and ensuring that military AI is developed and used responsibly.

This article examines the role of CBMs in managing the challenges posed by military AI. Drawing on lessons from arms control, international law, and AI governance, we propose a framework for designing effective CBMs tailored to the unique characteristics of AI technologies. We also discuss challenges to implementation and suggest pathways for institutionalizing these measures within broader frameworks for international security.

## The Strategic Importance of Confidence-Building Measures

CBMs have long been a cornerstone of international security, particularly in the context of conventional and nuclear arms control. These measures aim to reduce uncertainty and the potential for conflict by promoting transparency, communication, and verification among states. Historically, CBMs have included mechanisms such as arms reduction agreements, information-sharing protocols, and joint military exercises. 

With the advent of military AI, the logic behind CBMs remains compelling, but their design and implementation require adaptation to account for the distinct characteristics of AI systems:

1. **Opacity**: AI systems, particularly those based on machine learning, can behave unpredictably, making their intentions and capabilities harder to assess. This opacity can exacerbate mistrust between states.
2. **Dual-use technologies**: Many AI systems have both civilian and military applications, complicating efforts to track and regulate their development.
3. **Speed and autonomy**: AI systems can operate at speeds and levels of autonomy that challenge traditional concepts of human oversight and control, raising the risk of unintended escalation.

CBMs tailored to military AI can reduce these risks by fostering greater understanding and trust between states, thereby contributing to global stability and security.

## Key Elements of Confidence-Building Measures for Military AI

To be effective, CBMs for military AI should be designed with the following key elements in mind:

### 1. **Transparency and Information Sharing**

Transparency is a foundational component of CBMs. States should establish mechanisms to share relevant information about their military AI capabilities, development processes, and deployment policies. This could include:

- **National AI strategies**: Publishing high-level documents that outline a state's approach to military AI development and use can signal intent and reassure other states.
- **Technical standards and benchmarks**: Sharing information about the performance, limitations, and testing protocols of military AI systems can help other states assess their capabilities and risks. For example, efforts to develop safety benchmarks for AI systems have been discussed in [Benchmarking AI Safety: Beyond Capability Evaluations](/research/144-benchmarking-ai-safety-beyond-capability-evaluatio).
- **Incident reporting**: Developing shared platforms for reporting incidents involving military AI could help identify emerging risks and prevent escalation, as explored in [Incident Databases: Standardizing AI Harm Reporting](/research/142-incident-databases-standardizing-ai-harm-reporting).

### 2. **Verification Mechanisms**

Verification is essential for ensuring compliance with CBMs, but the verification of military AI presents unique challenges. Unlike traditional arms control, where physical inspections can confirm the presence or absence of weapons, AI systems are primarily software-based and can be easily concealed or reconfigured. Potential solutions include:

- **Algorithmic audits**: Independent audits of AI systems, conducted under agreed-upon protocols, could verify compliance with CBMs.
- **Access to training data**: Providing access to the datasets used to train military AI systems could help verify their intended use and identify potential biases or misuse.
- **Inspection of hardware facilities**: Monitoring the physical infrastructure used for AI development, such as data centers and supercomputers, could provide indirect evidence of military AI activities.

### 3. **Norms and Standards for Responsible Use**

In addition to transparency and verification, CBMs should promote shared norms and standards for the responsible use of military AI. These could include:

- **Prohibitions on specific applications**: For instance, there is growing international support for banning fully autonomous weapons, also known as "killer robots", as discussed in [Autonomous Weapons: The State of Treaty Negotiations](/research/161-autonomous-weapons-the-state-of-treaty-negotiation).
- **Requirements for human oversight**: Establishing and operationalizing the principle of "meaningful human control" over military AI systems can reduce the risk of unintended actions, as explored in [Meaningful Human Control: Operationalizing the Standard](/research/162-meaningful-human-control-operationalizing-the-stan).
- **Ethical guidelines**: Developing and adopting ethical guidelines for military AI, grounded in international humanitarian law, can provide a normative framework for assessing and regulating new technologies.

### 4. **Bilateral and Multilateral Dialogues**

Formal and informal dialogues between states are crucial for building trust and addressing concerns about military AI. These dialogues can take various forms:

- **Track 1 diplomacy**: Official negotiations and agreements between states can establish binding commitments to CBMs.
- **Track 2 diplomacy**: Informal discussions between academics, think tanks, and retired military officials can help build understanding and generate innovative solutions.
- **Multilateral forums**: Platforms such as the United Nations, NATO, and regional organizations can facilitate the development of international norms and agreements on military AI.

### 5. **Technological Solutions for Risk Mitigation**

Technological innovations can complement traditional CBMs by reducing the risks associated with military AI. For example:

- **Explainable AI (XAI)**: Developing AI systems with transparent decision-making processes can enhance trust and accountability.
- **Fail-safes and kill-switches**: Embedding mechanisms to halt or override AI systems in case of malfunction or escalation can provide an additional layer of security.
- **Simulation and wargaming**: Using virtual environments to simulate the behavior of military AI systems under various scenarios can help identify potential risks and inform the development of effective CBMs.

## Challenges to Implementation

Despite their potential benefits, CBMs for military AI face a number of challenges:

1. **Sovereignty concerns**: States may be reluctant to share information or allow inspections, fearing that this could compromise their security or technological edge.
2. **Attribution difficulties**: Identifying the source of an AI-enabled attack can be challenging, complicating efforts to hold states accountable for violations of CBMs.
3. **Technological complexity**: The technical nature of AI systems can make it difficult for policymakers and stakeholders to understand and evaluate their risks.
4. **Asymmetric capabilities**: Differences in technological capabilities between states can create power imbalances and complicate negotiations over CBMs.

## Pathways for Building Trust in Military AI

To address these challenges and enhance the effectiveness of CBMs, the following strategies should be considered:

1. **Capacity-building initiatives**: Providing technical assistance and resources to states with limited AI capabilities can reduce asymmetries and build trust.
2. **Public-private partnerships**: Engaging with technology companies, research institutions, and civil society organizations can help develop and implement effective CBMs.
3. **Incremental approaches**: Starting with voluntary and non-binding measures can build momentum for more robust CBMs in the future.
4. **Incentives for compliance**: Linking CBMs to tangible benefits, such as access to funding or technology, can encourage states to participate and comply.

## Conclusion

As AI continues to transform military capabilities, confidence-building measures will be essential for managing the associated risks and ensuring that these technologies are used responsibly. By fostering transparency, promoting shared norms, and leveraging both diplomatic and technological tools, states can reduce mistrust and enhance international security. While significant challenges remain, the development and implementation of CBMs offer a promising pathway toward a more stable and cooperative global order.

*This article focuses on the theoretical and policy dimensions of confidence-building measures for military AI. Future research should explore specific case studies and empirical analyses to evaluate the effectiveness of proposed measures.*
