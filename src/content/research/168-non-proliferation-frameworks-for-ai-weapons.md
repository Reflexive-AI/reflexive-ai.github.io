## Introduction

The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence poses unprecedented challenges to global security. Among the most pressing concerns is the potential proliferation of AI-enabled weapons: systems that use machine learning or other AI techniques to enhance targeting, decision-making, or autonomous action in combat scenarios. These technologies, often referred to as lethal autonomous weapon systems (LAWS), are not only reshaping traditional military capabilities but also raising profound ethical and strategic dilemmas. Unlike nuclear, biological, or chemical weapons, AI systems are software-based, dual-use, and relatively easy to replicate, making their proliferation difficult to control.

This article explores the concept of a non-proliferation framework for AI weapons, examining its feasibility, the lessons from historical arms control regimes, and the unique attributes of AI that complicate regulation. We argue that while a universal ban on AI weapons may be unlikely in the short term, carefully designed governance mechanisms can reduce the risks of uncontrolled proliferation and the resulting destabilization of international security.

## The Urgency of AI Non-Proliferation

The proliferation of AI weapons is not a distant concern: it is already underway. Militaries around the world are investing heavily in AI technologies to enhance intelligence gathering, decision-making, and lethality. These systems range from semi-autonomous drones to fully autonomous targeting systems. For example, in the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict in 2020, loitering munitions (commonly referred to as "kamikaze drones") were deployed with significant effect, demonstrating the transformative potential of AI-driven weaponry on modern battlefields.

The risks associated with AI weapon proliferation extend beyond military competition. These systems could destabilize international security by lowering the threshold for conflict. Autonomous weapons, for instance, could enable pre-emptive strikes without human intervention, increasing the risk of accidental escalation, as discussed in [Escalation Risks from Autonomous Military Systems](/research/167-escalation-risks-from-autonomous-military-systems). Moreover, the dual-use nature of AI makes it exceptionally difficult to control: the same algorithms used for civilian applications, such as object recognition, can also be repurposed for automated targeting.

## Lessons from Historical Non-Proliferation Frameworks

Efforts to prevent the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction (WMDs) provide important lessons for designing a non-proliferation framework for AI weapons. Treaties like the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT), the Chemical Weapons Convention (CWC), and the Biological Weapons Convention (BWC) demonstrate the potential of multilateral agreements to reduce global risks. However, these frameworks also highlight the challenges of enforcement, verification, and universal adherence.

### Key Lessons

1. **Verification Mechanisms**: Effective non-proliferation agreements require robust verification mechanisms to ensure compliance. For example, the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) conducts inspections to verify that nuclear materials are not diverted for military purposes. Applying similar methods to AI systems, however, is far more complex due to the intangible nature of software.

2. **Dual-Use Dilemmas**: The CWC and BWC grapple with the dual-use nature of chemical and biological agents, which have legitimate civilian applications but can also be weaponized. AI faces an even more pronounced dual-use challenge: algorithms developed for benign purposes, such as autonomous navigation in vehicles, can easily be adapted for military use.

3. **Norm-Building**: Non-proliferation frameworks rely heavily on the establishment of international norms. The norm against the use of chemical weapons, for instance, has been reinforced through decades of condemnation and limited use. Similarly, the stigmatization of AI weapons could play a critical role in reducing their proliferation.

### Limitations of Historical Models

While historical frameworks provide valuable insights, they also reveal limitations when applied to AI. Unlike nuclear materials, which are physically scarce and require significant infrastructure to develop, AI systems can be created and distributed with minimal resources. This decentralization makes traditional monitoring and enforcement strategies less effective. Furthermore, the lack of a clear definition of what constitutes an "AI weapon" complicates efforts to build consensus around regulation.

## Core Components of an AI Non-Proliferation Framework

Designing a framework to prevent the proliferation of AI weapons requires addressing the technology's unique characteristics. Below, we outline several core components that could form the basis of such a framework.

### 1. **Definitional Clarity**

A major challenge in AI non-proliferation is defining what constitutes an AI weapon. Should the term apply only to fully autonomous systems, or should it include semi-autonomous systems with AI-enhanced decision-making? A narrow definition risks excluding emerging threats, while an overly broad definition could hinder legitimate research and development.

### 2. **Dual-Use Governance**

To address the dual-use nature of AI, any non-proliferation framework must differentiate between legitimate civilian applications and potential military misuse. This could involve the establishment of licensing regimes for certain high-risk AI technologies, similar to export control frameworks for advanced semiconductors.

### 3. **Transparency and Accountability**

Transparency measures, such as requiring states to disclose their development and deployment of AI weapons, could reduce the risk of accidental escalation. However, implementing such measures would require international cooperation and trust. An AI-specific "incident database," as proposed in [Incident Databases: Standardizing AI Harm Reporting](/research/142-incident-databases-standardizing-ai-harm-reporting), could also help track and analyze misuse cases to inform policy.

### 4. **Norms and Stigmatization**

International norms against the development and use of AI weapons could be a powerful tool for non-proliferation. By stigmatizing certain applications—such as fully autonomous systems capable of lethal force without meaningful human control—states could reduce the incentive to develop and deploy these technologies. The concept of meaningful human control is further explored in [Meaningful Human Control: Operationalizing the Standard](/research/162-meaningful-human-control-operationalizing-the-stan).

### 5. **Enforcement and Verification**

Enforcing a non-proliferation framework for AI weapons will require innovative approaches to verification. Traditional methods, such as on-site inspections, may not be feasible for software-based systems. Instead, international agreements could mandate the use of audit trails, secure logs, or cryptographic proofs to verify compliance, as discussed in [Audit Trail Analysis: Detecting Governance Failures](/research/146-audit-trail-analysis-detecting-governance-failures).

## Challenges to Implementation

Despite its potential, the creation of a non-proliferation framework for AI weapons faces significant obstacles. The lack of consensus among major powers, the rapid pace of technological innovation, and the difficulty of enforcement all pose serious challenges. Additionally, geopolitical tensions could undermine efforts to negotiate and implement such a framework.

One of the most significant barriers is the asymmetry in AI capabilities among nations. While some states possess advanced AI research ecosystems, others are still developing the necessary infrastructure. This asymmetry could lead to a fragmented regulatory landscape, with powerful states resisting constraints that might limit their competitive advantage.

## Toward a Multilateral Approach

Given these challenges, a multilateral approach is essential. International organizations such as the United Nations or regional coalitions like the European Union could play a central role in convening negotiations and building consensus. Track II diplomacy—informal dialogues involving academics, industry leaders, and civil society—could also contribute to the development of norms and best practices.

Ultimately, the success of any non-proliferation framework will depend on the willingness of states to prioritize long-term stability over short-term strategic gains. This requires not only technical innovation but also political resolve and ethical leadership.

## Conclusion

The proliferation of AI weapons represents one of the most urgent challenges in contemporary international security. While the unique characteristics of AI complicate traditional non-proliferation approaches, carefully designed frameworks that incorporate transparency, norms, and innovative verification mechanisms can mitigate the risks. Drawing from historical precedents while addressing the specificities of AI, the international community must act decisively to prevent the destabilizing effects of unrestricted AI weapon proliferation.

*This article focuses on the conceptual groundwork for AI non-proliferation and does not address the technical specifics of individual AI systems or their operational use cases. Future research is needed to explore detailed verification methods and the implications of emerging technologies.*
